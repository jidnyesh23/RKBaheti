GST Audit Checklist:
Smooth & Penalty-Free Assessment

Step-by-step checklist to ensure your books are audit-ready. Essential points every taxpayer must verify before a GST department audit.

CA Rajesh Sharma February 10, 2026 10 min read
Back to Knowledge Bank
AUDIT CA Rajesh Sharma February 10, 2026 10 min read

With the GST department deploying data analytics, ASMT-10 scrutiny notices, and special audit teams under Section 66, the probability of your business facing a GST audit or verification has never been higher. Being unprepared can cost you dearly — in terms of demands, penalties, and interest. This comprehensive checklist helps you stay audit-ready throughout the year.

"A GST audit is not something that happens to you — it is something you should be prepared for at all times. The 15 points in this checklist can be the difference between a clean chit and a tax demand."

Understanding GST Audits — Types & Triggers

Before diving into the checklist, it's important to understand what kind of audit you may face:

  • Departmental Audit (Section 65): Conducted by GST officers at your place of business. Triggered by desk scrutiny mismatches, tip-offs, or random selection.
  • Special Audit (Section 66): Ordered by a commissioner if the officer believes a CA/CMA audit is required. Very intrusive and can result in significant demands.
  • Annual Return Scrutiny (Section 61): Based on discrepancies between GSTR-1, GSTR-3B, and GSTR-9.
  • ASMT-10 Notice: Automated notice based on data analytics comparing purchase and sale patterns.

The 15-Point GST Audit Checklist

A. Books of Accounts & Records

1. Maintain all prescribed records under Rule 56 Ensure purchase register, sales register, stock register, ITC register, and output tax liability register are maintained digitally or physically in the prescribed format.
2. Retain records for 6 years from the due date of Annual Return GST records must be preserved for a minimum of 6 years. For FY 2018-19, the earliest year, records should be retained till December 2025.
3. Reconcile books of accounts with GST returns monthly Any difference between accounting ledgers and GSTR-1/3B filed figures must be documented and explainable as timing differences, rounding, or adjustments.

B. Input Tax Credit (ITC) Verification

4. Verify all ITC against GSTR-2B Confirm that every rupee of ITC claimed in GSTR-3B is reflected in GSTR-2B. Excess ITC claims are the #1 cause of GST demands.
5. Reverse ITC on blocked credits (Section 17(5)) Check for ITC claimed on motor vehicles (non-eligible), construction expenses, personal consumption, and club/membership subscriptions — all blocked under Section 17(5).
6. Apply proportionate ITC reversal for exempt/non-GST supplies (Rule 42/43) If you make both taxable and exempt supplies, proportionate ITC must be reversed. Ensure Rule 42 calculations are done monthly and Rule 43 for capital goods annually.
7. Check for ITC on creditors outstanding for more than 180 days Under Rule 37, if payment to a supplier is not made within 180 days of invoice, ITC must be reversed along with interest.

C. Outward Supply Compliance

8. Ensure GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B are reconciled with books The turnover reported in GSTR-1, GSTR-3B, and your audited financial statement must match. Any difference requires clear documentation.
9. Verify HSN/SAC classification for all major supplies Mis-classification of goods/services leading to lower tax rates is a primary target of audit teams. Ensure HSN codes map to the correct GST rate.
10. Account for all deemed/non-cash supplies Barter transactions, free samples above ₹50 per recipient, stock transfers between states, and related-party transactions at non-market prices need to be correctly taxed.

D. Refunds, Advances & Export Compliance

11. Reconcile advances received vs. GST paid Tax on advances received for goods/services must be discharged in the period of receipt (unless covered by notification for goods). Maintain an advance register.
12. Export documentation must be watertight All zero-rated export invoices must be backed by shipping bills, BRC (Bank Realisation Certificate), LUT/Bond reference, and customs acknowledgements.

E. Return Filing & E-Way Bill Compliance

13. Ensure GSTR-9 and GSTR-9C are consistent Annual return figures must reconcile with the audited financials and monthly/quarterly returns. Any differences need proper disclosures in Table 9/10/11.
14. Verify e-Way Bill compliance for all consignments above ₹50,000 Maintain a log of all e-Way bills generated and ensure they match your sales register. Expired or missing e-Way bills are a frequent audit trigger for goods movements.
15. Conduct an internal GST health check every quarter Don't wait for the auditor to identify problems. An internal quarterly review of the above points significantly reduces audit risk and ensures timely self-corrections.
Penalty Provisions You Must Know

Under GST law, non-compliance can attract penalties ranging from ₹10,000 to 100% of tax evaded. Interest at 18% p.a. (24% for incorrect ITC) accrues from the date the tax was due. Section 74 (fraud) provisions can result in penalty equal to the tax amount plus prosecution in serious cases.

What to Expect During a Section 65 Audit

When a GST officer visits your premises for a departmental audit, here's what typically happens:

  1. Officer issues a notice at least 15 working days prior, specifying the period and tax period to be audited.
  2. You must provide access to books of accounts, returns filed, tax payment records, and other documents.
  3. The audit must be completed within 3 months (extendable to 6 months by the commissioner).
  4. A final audit report is issued. If discrepancies are found, a Show Cause Notice (SCN) under Section 73 or 74 follows.
  5. You have the right to be heard before any demand is confirmed.

Pro tip: When a GST audit notice arrives, engage a GST professional immediately. Do not respond to the audit team without legal representation — every communication becomes part of the record.
