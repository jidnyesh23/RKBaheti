Key GST Amendments &
Compliance Updates for FY 2025-26

A comprehensive overview of the latest GST council decisions and their impact on businesses — from rate changes to new filing requirements.

CA R.K. Baheti February 20, 2026 8 min read
Back to Knowledge Bank
GST CA R.K. Baheti February 20, 2026 8 min read

The GST landscape in India continues to evolve rapidly. The 55th and 56th GST Council meetings held in December 2025 and January 2026 respectively have introduced a series of amendments that every registered taxpayer, business owner, and finance professional needs to act on promptly. This article summarises the key changes and their practical implications for FY 2025-26.

"GST compliance is no longer optional — with increased AI-driven scrutiny from the GST department, real-time reconciliation and accurate filing are now a business necessity."

1. Rate Rationalisation — What Changed?

The council approved sweeping rate changes aimed at simplifying the four-slab structure. Effective 1 April 2026, the following notable rate revisions apply:

Category Old Rate New Rate Effective Date
Ready-to-eat packaged food (branded) 12% 5% 01 Apr 2026
Health insurance premiums (senior citizens) 18% Nil 01 Apr 2026
Term life insurance premiums 18% Nil 01 Apr 2026
Electric vehicles (commercial) 12% 5% 01 Apr 2026
Online gaming (actionable claims) 28% 28% (confirmed) Ongoing

These changes require businesses to update their ERP/POS systems immediately and reconfigure HSN-wise tax rates in GSTN records.

2. Input Tax Credit (ITC) Rule Amendments

Rule 36(4) under the CGST Rules has been amended to make ITC matching stricter. Key changes include:

  • 100% ITC restriction on inwards supplies not reflected in GSTR-2B unless supplier-level reconciliation is provided.
  • The provisional ITC credit of 5% (earlier 10%) has been completely removed. Only GSTR-2B reflected ITC is now claimable.
  • ITC on capital goods forming part of a Works Contract is blocked — a clarification issued via Circular No. 205/17/2024-GST reaffirmed this position.
  • Businesses must ensure suppliers file their GSTR-1/IFF on time or follow-up mechanisms are in place.

Practical Tip

Establish a monthly vendor compliance tracker. Use the GSTR-2B auto-drafted data as the base for ITC claims. Reconcile purchase registers with GSTR-2B at least 7 days before filing GSTR-3B to allow time for supplier follow-ups.

3. E-Invoicing Threshold Lowered

The mandatory e-invoicing threshold has been revised. Businesses with aggregate turnover exceeding ₹5 crore (down from ₹10 crore) in any preceding financial year are now required to generate e-invoices for all B2B supplies. The timeline for implementation is 1 April 2026.

Turnover Threshold Mandatory E-Invoicing From
Above ₹500 crore 01 Oct 2020
Above ₹100 crore 01 Jan 2021
Above ₹50 crore 01 Apr 2021
Above ₹10 crore 01 Oct 2022
Above ₹5 crore 01 Aug 2023
All B2B taxpayers (proposed) 01 Apr 2027 (expected)

Non-compliance with e-invoicing requirements can result in denial of ITC to the recipient and penalties up to ₹25,000 per invoice for the supplier.

4. New GSTR-1A — Amend Before GSTR-3B

A new optional form, GSTR-1A, has been introduced to allow suppliers to amend details of outward supplies filed in GSTR-1/IFF before filing GSTR-3B for the same period. This bridges the earlier gap where errors could only be corrected in subsequent months. Key implications:

  • Amendments flow directly to the recipient's GSTR-2B before GSTR-3B filing.
  • Increases the accuracy of net tax liability computation in GSTR-3B.
  • Highly recommended for businesses with high volumes of credit notes or invoice amendments.

5. New Invoice Management System (IMS)

The Invoice Management System (IMS), launched in October 2024, is now a critical part of the GST ecosystem. Taxpayers can accept, reject, or keep invoices pending on the IMS dashboard, which directly affects GSTR-2B population. Important points:

  • Invoices not acted upon are deemed accepted and populate GSTR-2B automatically.
  • Rejected invoices are communicated back to the supplier for rectification.
  • IMS data forms the basis for scrutiny notices under Section 61.

Businesses should designate a responsible GST officer to review the IMS dashboard monthly, before the GSTR-2B generation date (14th of every month).

6. Compliance Calendar for FY 2025-26 — Key Deadlines

Return Frequency Due Date
GSTR-1 Monthly (TO > ₹5 Cr) 11th of next month
GSTR-1 (QRMP) Quarterly 13th of month following quarter
GSTR-3B Monthly (TO > ₹5 Cr) 20th of next month
GSTR-3B (QRMP) Quarterly 22nd / 24th of month following quarter
GSTR-9 (Annual) Annual 31st December
GSTR-9C (Reconciliation) Annual (TO > ₹5 Cr) 31st December

Action Checklist for Businesses

  • Update HSN tax rates in accounting/ERP systems for revised rate structure.
  • Implement e-invoicing if turnover exceeds ₹5 crore.
  • Stop claiming provisional ITC — restrict claims to GSTR-2B only.
  • Review GSTR-1A workflow for ongoing corrections before GSTR-3B filing.
  • Designate an officer for monthly IMS dashboard review.
  • Begin GSTR-9 / GSTR-9C preparation from Q3 itself to avoid last-minute rush.
